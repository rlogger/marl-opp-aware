\large A controlled predator--prey study: inferring a varying opponent's\\
latent strategy and planning against it}
\author{Rajdeep Singh \\ REL Lab, University of Southern California}
\date{May 2026}

\begin{abstract}
In adversarial co-training each team faces an opponent whose strategy varies and
adapts; a best response that overfits to one opponent loses robustness to
strategies it has not recently seen. The remedy we pursue is an
\emph{uncertainty-aware} model of the opponent's latent strategy (Part~1),
\emph{sampled inside a model-based planner} (Part~2). This note instantiates and
validates both parts in a controlled predator--prey game. The opponent---the prey,
team \red{red}---draws a hidden strategy each episode; the controlled team---the
predators, team \blue{blue}---cannot observe it and must infer it from
\red{red}'s behavior, then plan. We instantiate the latent strategy
$\zlat$ as a discrete intent so inference can be scored against ground truth.
We find: \textbf{(i)} the opponent's latent strategy is decisive---a \blue{blue}
policy that observes $\zlat$ does $+51\%$; \textbf{(ii)} it is inferable from
\red{red}'s trajectory with calibrated uncertainty---an encoder
$q_\phi(\zlat\mid\tred)$ recovers it with accuracy $0.37\!\to\!0.97$ over
$3\!\to\!25$ observed steps as its posterior entropy collapses; \textbf{(iii)} a
\emph{point-estimate} opponent model is brittle---fed a wrong guess it loses
$47\%$, exactly the failure uncertainty-aware modeling is meant to prevent; and
\textbf{(iv)} sampling the uncertainty-aware opponent model inside a planner
yields the most robust best response, $+61\%$ over an opponent-blind baseline and
\emph{above an oracle} \blue{blue} policy told the true strategy. An ablation that
flattens the belief shows the inference, not the lookahead, is the active
ingredient ($+40\%$). Finally, we replace the supervised encoder with a
self-supervised \emph{predictive} one (JEPA): it recovers the opponent's strategy
far better than a generative VAE ($0.89$ vs.\ $0.53$ probe; $0.65$ vs.\ $0.14$
ARI) and from about half the observation, and---driving the planner with a
\emph{label-free} belief---it matches the supervised pipeline ($4.08$ vs.\ $4.31$
captures) with no opponent-strategy labels at all. The study validates the
proposal's Part~1/Part~2 loop on its first target domain and shows a predictive,
label-free encoder is the stronger Part~1.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background: adaptive opponent modeling for co-training}

\textbf{Adversarial co-training}---training both teams together rather than
scripting one---is necessary for robust strategies in complex games, but it is
hard: each team faces a \emph{non-stationary} opponent, policies can fall into
cyclic (rock--paper--scissors) dynamics, and a team readily \emph{overfits to the
current opponent}, losing to strategies that have not appeared recently. Existing
approaches under-serve exactly the opponent that varies at test time: league play
is brute force and brittle to novel opponents; CTDE methods (e.g.\ MADDPG) assume
centralized access to the opponent and model \emph{no} uncertainty in its
strategy; model-based opponent modeling assumes known goals or clones behavior to
a moving target.

\textbf{The approach.} Model the opponent's strategy with \emph{calibrated
uncertainty} and \emph{plan} against that model. Two coupled parts:
\begin{description}[style=nextline,font=\normalfont\bfseries,leftmargin=1.2em]
\item[Part 1 (uncertainty-aware opponent model).] A variational encoder
$q_\phi(\zlat \mid \tred)$ maps the opponent's (\red{red}'s) trajectory to a
latent strategy $\zlat$ with a posterior that expresses how sure we are; a
latent-conditioned opponent policy $\pi^{\mathrm{red}}(a \mid s, \zlat)$
reconstructs \red{red}'s behavior (by behavior cloning or contrastive preference
learning). The model must stay robust to opponents that adapt and use varying
strategies.
\item[Part 2 (plan against it).] The controlled team (\blue{blue}) plans with a
model-based search that \emph{samples} the opponent's moves from
$\pi^{\mathrm{red}}(\cdot \mid s, \zlat)$ and conditions its value
$v^{\mathrm{blue}}(s, \zlat)$ on the inferred strategy, so the plan is aware of
which opponent it currently faces.
\end{description}

\textbf{This study.} We validate the loop in the proposal's first domain,
predator--prey, in a form where every quantity can be measured. The opponent
(prey, \red{red}) is assigned a hidden \emph{intent} each episode---one of four
corners it is rewarded for haunting---which the controlled predators (\blue{blue})
cannot observe. The intent is the latent strategy $\zlat$, made discrete so the
encoder's belief can be checked against truth and so ``the opponent uses varying
strategies'' has an exact meaning: a fresh $\zlat$ every episode. The environment
is otherwise fixed, so $\zlat$ is \emph{not} readable from a single observation
and can only be had by modeling \red{red}'s behavior over time.

\section{Setup}

Three \blue{blue} predators $P$ pursue one \red{red} prey $q$ in a fixed $2$D
arena (JaxMARL MPE, five discrete actions, $25$-step episodes, two fixed
obstacles). At reset the prey draws $\zlat \sim \mathrm{Uniform}\{0,1,2,3\}$
indexing a corner $c_\zlat \in \{\pm 0.8\}^2$. With $k^t_i = \mathbf{1}[\lVert
x^t_i - x^t_q\rVert < \rho_i+\rho_q]$ marking predator $i$ in contact with the
prey,
\begin{equation}
  r^{\mathrm{\blue{blue}},t} = \lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}\!\sum_{i\in P} k^t_i,
  \qquad
  r^{\mathrm{\red{red}},t} = -\lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}\!\sum_{i\in P} k^t_i
     + \lambda_{\mathrm{int}}\,\mathbf{1}\!\big[\lVert x^t_q - c_\zlat \rVert < r_{\mathrm{int}}\big],
\end{equation}
$\lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}{=}10$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{int}}{=}4$, $r_{\mathrm{int}}{=}0.35$:
the prey loiters at its assigned corner when safe while evading. The prey observes
its own $\zlat$; \emph{the predators do not}. Each policy is a MAPPO co-training
run (centralized critic, $3$ seeds); we report captures per episode,
$\mathrm{cap}=\lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}^{-1}\sum_t r^{\mathrm{\blue{blue}},t}$, over
$300$ episodes.

\section{Method (the controlled instantiation)}

\paragraph{Part 1 --- an uncertainty-aware model of the opponent's strategy.}
From the prey's first $k$ positions $\tred_{0:k}$ we fit
$q_\phi(\zlat \mid \tred_{0:k})$, here a classifier whose softmax is a posterior
belief $\bel_k$ over the four latent strategies. The belief, not a point estimate,
is the object we carry: it sharpens online as more of the opponent is observed,
which is the discrete instance of the proposal's variational posterior over
$\zlat$. The opponent policy model is $\pi^{\mathrm{red}}(a\mid s,\zlat)$, the
prey's own behavior under strategy $\zlat$ (the target a latent-conditioned BC/CPL
model is trained to reproduce).

\paragraph{Part 2 --- planning against the uncertainty-aware model.}
At state $s_t$ with belief $\bel_t$, \blue{blue} scores each candidate joint action
$u$ by a short rollout in which \red{red}'s moves are \emph{sampled from the
opponent model under a strategy drawn from the belief}:
\begin{equation}
  Q(s_t,u) = \expect_{\zlat\sim\bel_t}\!\left[
  \sum_{h=0}^{H-1}\gamma^h r^{\mathrm{\blue{blue}}}_{t+h}
  \;-\; w\,\min_{i\in P}\lVert x^{t+H}_i - x^{t+H}_q\rVert
  \;\middle|\; u_t{=}u,\; a^q_{t+h}\!\sim\!\pi^{\mathrm{red}}(\cdot\,|\,s_{t+h},\zlat)\right],
  \label{eq:plan}
\end{equation}
and acts $u_t=\arg\max_u Q(s_t,u)$ ($H{=}5$, $\gamma{=}0.95$, $K{=}8$ rollouts,
leaf weight $w{=}1$, strategies shared across candidates). Marginalizing over $\bel_t$ makes the plan
\emph{uncertainty-aware}: while the belief is flat \blue{blue} hedges, and as it
sharpens \blue{blue} commits. The leaf term rewards ending near the imagined
future prey, which under a sampled strategy sits at the believed corner---an
interception. This is the simulator-based instance of the proposal's
tree search that samples $\pi^{\mathrm{red}}(\cdot\mid\zlat)$ at each node and
scores with a strategy-aware value $v^{\mathrm{blue}}(s,\zlat)$.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{(i) The opponent's latent strategy is inferable (Table~\ref{tab:enc}, Figure~\ref{fig:overview}).}
The prey's motion clusters by strategy (Figure~\ref{fig:overview}, left), and the
encoder recovers $\zlat$ with accuracy $0.37\!\to\!0.97$ over $3\!\to\!25$ steps as
its posterior entropy collapses from $1.35$ to $0.03$ nats---a usable,
increasingly-confident belief well before the episode ends.

\paragraph{(ii) The strategy is decisive, and a point estimate is brittle.}
A \blue{blue} policy that observes the true $\zlat$ catches the prey $4.05$
times/episode vs.\ $2.68$ for an opponent-blind baseline ($+51\%$): knowing which
opponent one faces matters a great deal. But a \blue{blue} policy trained on
\emph{perfect} $\zlat$ and fed a \emph{wrong} one collapses to $1.42$
($-47\%$)---the precise brittleness of opponent models that ignore their own
uncertainty, which the uncertainty-aware belief exists to prevent.

\paragraph{(iii) Planning against the uncertainty-aware model is the most robust
response (Table~\ref{tab:main}, Figure~\ref{fig:plan}).}
The planner of Eq.~\eqref{eq:plan} reaches $\mathbf{4.31}$ captures: $+61\%$ over
the opponent-blind baseline, $+53\%$ over a reactive policy given the same belief,
and \emph{above the oracle reactive policy} ($4.05$)---planning interceptions
against the inferred opponent beats reacting even with the true strategy in hand.
The control isolates the cause: a planner given a \emph{flat} belief (lookahead,
no opponent inference) reaches only $3.07$, so the inferred opponent model
supplies $+40\%$. The opponent inference, not the lookahead, is what wins.

\section{Part 1 without labels: predict, don't reconstruct}

The encoder above used intent labels. A Part~1 that scales should recover the
opponent's strategy \emph{self-supervised}, from trajectories alone---and the
\emph{objective} matters. We compare two self-supervised encoders of the prey
trajectory on identical data, both bottlenecked to a $2$-D latent (so the latent
\emph{is} the figure) and scored only afterward against the four ground-truth
intents:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=1.4em]
\item \textbf{VAE (generative):} encode the first $12$ steps, then
\emph{reconstruct} them (ELBO). It must model every detail---the random start and
the evasion noise included.
\item \textbf{JEPA (predictive):} encode the first $12$ steps, then \emph{predict
the representation} of the future window (steps $12$--$24$, the corner approach)
through an EMA target encoder---no reconstruction. It keeps only the predictable
structure (where the prey is heading $=$ its strategy) and discards the noise.
\end{itemize}

  (4-way; probe chance $0.25$; mean $\pm$ std over $3$ encoder seeds). The
  predictive encoder wins on both metrics and beats a supervised classifier
  trained on the raw window.}\label{tab:jepa}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
    \toprule
    self-supervised encoder & probe acc. & GMM ARI \\
    \midrule
    VAE (generative, reconstruct)              & $0.53 \pm 0.00$ & $0.14 \pm 0.01$ \\
    \textbf{JEPA (predictive, predict repr.)}  & $\mathbf{0.89 \pm 0.01}$ & $\mathbf{0.65 \pm 0.08}$ \\
    \midrule
    \emph{supervised classifier (raw window)}  & $0.85$ & --- \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The predictive objective recovers the opponent's strategy where reconstruction
cannot (Table~\ref{tab:jepa}, Figure~\ref{fig:jepa})---and, notably, its smooth
$2$-D latent is \emph{more} linearly separable than the raw window a supervised
classifier sees. This is LeCun's JEPA principle (predict in representation space,
not input space; cf.\ V-JEPA~2~\cite{assran2025vjepa2}) applied to opponent
modeling: the part of an
opponent's behavior worth encoding is the part you can \emph{predict}, and a
generative encoder squanders capacity on the rest.

\paragraph{An anytime opponent encoder.}
Because it predicts where the prey is heading instead of waiting for it to
arrive, the JEPA encoder also reads the strategy from \emph{fewer} observed
steps. Sweeping the observation budget $k$ (observe the first $k$ steps of the
window, mask and predict the future), the predictive encoder dominates the
generative one at every $k$ (Figure~\ref{fig:jepaspeed}): at $k{=}11$ steps it
already recovers the intent at $0.80$---what the VAE needs $k{=}20$ for. A planner
can therefore commit roughly twice as soon.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../plots/jepa_inference_speed.png}
  \caption{Intent recovery vs.\ steps of the opponent observed (3-seed mean
  $\pm$ std). The predictive (JEPA) encoder reads the strategy from about half the
  observation of the generative (VAE) one, tracking just under the supervised
  ceiling.}
  \label{fig:jepaspeed}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{It closes the loop, without labels.}
Finally we drive the Part~2 planner with a \emph{fully self-supervised} belief: a
JEPA encoder, a readout $z \mapsto$ predicted arrival position (trained on the
prey's own future positions---no intent labels), and a posterior over the four
\emph{known} corners, $b(c) \propto \exp(-\lVert \mathrm{readout}(z) - c_g\rVert^2/\sigma)$.
This drops into the planner's belief slot unchanged. With no opponent-strategy
labels anywhere, it reaches $\mathbf{4.08 \pm 0.57}$ captures/episode
(Figure~\ref{fig:jepaplan})---matching the supervised-belief planner
($4.31$, overlapping error bars) and above the oracle reactive predator
($4.05$). The labels the rest of the pipeline used are not, in fact, necessary:
a predictive self-supervised opponent model recovers the same best response.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{../plots/part2_jepa_planner.png}
  \caption{Label-free opponent-aware planning. A self-supervised JEPA belief
  drives the planner to the supervised result without any intent labels.}
  \label{fig:jepaplan}
\end{figure}

This is the cleaner, more general Part~1 for the proposal: no labels, a latent
that recovers strategy faster and drops straight into the belief and the planner,
and a downstream best response that matches the supervised pipeline.

\section{The second strategy axis: circle vs.\ corners}
\label{sec:resources}

The hidden-intent task has a four-way \emph{discrete} strategy. The lab's other
axis is induced by the environment: a prey trained with resources placed on a
circle (radius $0.6$) routes differently from one trained with resources in the
corners ($\sim$$1.13$). We ask the same Part-1 question there: can an
unsupervised encoder recover \emph{which specialist} it is watching? (Specialists
are rolled out in their native environments against the weakened predators they
were trained with; $1{,}200$ episodes, $3$ encoder seeds throughout.)

\paragraph{The signal is in occupancy, not in a trajectory window.}
A supervised probe on a raw absolute-coordinate window of the first episode is at
\emph{chance} ($0.54$ at $k{=}12$--$25$; chance $0.5$): no encoder, of any kind,
could separate the placements from that input, because a single episode is
dominated by evasion. The same probe on a normalized $8{\times}8$
\emph{occupancy histogram} --- \emph{where} the prey spends its time --- reaches
$0.71/0.88/0.93$ over $25/50/100$ observed steps. The featurization, not the
encoder objective, gates this task.

\paragraph{Predict vs.\ reconstruct, revisited.}
On occupancy features with a $2$-D latent, the JEPA encoder again leads the VAE
($0.63$ vs.\ $0.48$ probe at $k{=}50$; $0.67$ vs.\ $0.66$ at $k{=}100$), but with
a $4$-D latent the ordering \emph{reverses} ($0.81$ VAE vs.\ $0.67$ JEPA at
$k{=}100$). This delineates the principle from Part~1: JEPA's advantage comes
from \emph{discarding unpredictable detail}, which is decisive when the input
mixes evasion noise with signal (raw trajectories, Table~\ref{tab:jepa}) --- but
an occupancy histogram has \emph{already} aggregated the noise away, so
reconstruction stops paying a noise tax and capacity favors the generative
objective. Neither objective, at any tested configuration, produces
unsupervised \emph{clusters} (GMM ARI $\approx 0$, matching the earlier
occupancy-VAE study): the placement is a linearly decodable \emph{direction} in
latent space, not a pair of separated modes. Closing that gap is exactly where
return-relevant intent objectives~\cite{odrowaz2026gim} or
equivariant/contrastive shaping enter. The predators' own occupancy is far less
informative (supervised probe $0.64$ at $k{=}100$; latents near chance):
the \emph{opponent} is the signal carrier.

\paragraph{Does the latent help a predator model?}
Following the behaviour-cloning protocol, we predict a predator's next action
from all agent positions, $\pi(a\mid s)$, versus the same input plus the
unsupervised prey latent, $\pi(a\mid s, z)$ --- with $z$ computed from the
prey's \emph{first} episode and BC evaluated on the \emph{second} (episode-level
train/val split, $3$ seeds; Table~\ref{tab:bc}). Conditioning on the \emph{true}
placement helps clearly ($0.800 \pm 0.007$ vs.\ $0.776 \pm 0.004$ held-out
action accuracy), so the hypothesized effect is real; conditioning on today's
unsupervised latents does not ($0.772$ for both VAE and JEPA) --- exactly as the
probe curve predicts, since at a single episode of observation ($25$ steps)
those latents carry almost no placement signal ($\approx 0.51$ probe). The
oracle--baseline gap is the value a better opponent latent stands to capture.

\section{Related work}

\paragraph{Opponent modeling and theory of mind.}
Modeling other agents is a long-standing problem~\cite{albrecht2018}. Deep
approaches range from encoding opponent observations into a value
network~\cite{he2016} to meta-learning a prior that infers an agent's goals from
behavior alone~\cite{rabinowitz2018}, with recursive variants that reason about
what the opponent believes about us~\cite{wen2019} and recent attention-based
instantiations that infer other agents' goal-attention states~\cite{long2025iaa}.
Closest to our Part~1 is the
unsupervised opponent encoder of Papoudakis and Albrecht~\cite{papoudakis2019vae},
which models opponents with a variational autoencoder from local information.
Our predictive encoder targets exactly that setting and improves on it: a VAE
spends capacity reconstructing the prey's evasion noise, whereas a joint-embedding
predictor keeps only what is predictable---the strategy (probe accuracy $0.89$ vs
$0.53$, matching a supervised classifier). Reasoning about an \emph{uncertain}
opponent type is studied for imperfect-information games~\cite{shen2019}; our
belief-sharpness ablation sharpens the point---acting on a confident but wrong
type ($-47\%$) is worse than ignoring type altogether.

\paragraph{Non-stationarity and adaptive opponents.}
When opponents adapt, the learning target moves~\cite{hernandezleal2017}.
Meta-learning yields few-shot adaptation against changing
opponents~\cite{alshedivat2018}, online schemes detect and switch responses as
the opponent type changes mid-interaction~\cite{everett2018}, and recent work adds
\emph{active} context-aware exploration that gathers information to identify the
peer before exploiting it~\cite{ma2024fpa}. Our prey draws a
\emph{fixed} hidden intent per episode, so we study calibrated inference of a
static type rather than tracking an adaptive one; making the prey switch or
best-respond, and tracking it online, is the natural escalation and the regime in
which recursive reasoning~\cite{wen2019} and meta-adaptation~\cite{alshedivat2018}
become the right comparisons.

\paragraph{Model-based MARL and planning with a learned model.}
Planning with a learned model attains strong sample efficiency in single-agent
RL~\cite{schrittwieser2020,ye2021,hafner2020}, and recent work lifts this to
multiple agents---Dreamer-style world models~\cite{egorov2022}, multi-agent tree
search over a learned model~\cite{liu2024}, and opponent-aware model-based methods
that imagine improving opponent policies~\cite{yu2021} or build per-opponent
rollout models with sample-complexity guarantees~\cite{zhang2021}; transformer
world models now lead this line on sample efficiency~\cite{zhang2024marie,
deihim2025matwm}. Closest to our full pipeline, HOP~\cite{huang2024hop} couples a
Bayesian belief over other agents' goals---updated both within and across
episodes---with Monte-Carlo tree search and reports few-shot adaptation to unseen
opponents; we differ in the predictive, label-free opponent encoder and the
calibrated-uncertainty ablations. Our planner (simulator-based, and its
learned-model variant) is in this family but deliberately minimal: rather than
learn an opponent \emph{policy} or \emph{dynamics} model, we infer a
low-dimensional \emph{intent} belief and plan against it. We have not yet
benchmarked against
\cite{liu2024,egorov2022,yu2021,huang2024hop,deihim2025matwm}; doing
so---measuring episodes-to-recover against
a novel prey under limited interaction---is the experiment that would directly test
whether an explicit intent belief is more sample-efficient than a learned opponent
model, the central hypothesis of the broader project.

\paragraph{Self-predictive representations and JEPA.}
Predicting one's own future latent state via an EMA target encoder and a learned
transition model is a powerful auxiliary for sample-efficient
RL~\cite{schwarzer2021}, unified theoretically as self-predictive
abstraction~\cite{ni2024}; the joint-embedding predictive architecture applies the
same non-generative principle to images~\cite{assran2023}, to
trajectories~\cite{li2024tjepa}, and---action-conditioned---to video world models
that support planning directly in representation space~\cite{assran2025vjepa2}.
Our contribution is to transplant this machinery
from \emph{self}-modeling to \emph{opponent} modeling: the JEPA encoder predicts
the representation of the opponent's future motion, which we show is a better
strategy code than a reconstructive VAE and yields a label-free belief that matches
the supervised pipeline.

\paragraph{Symmetry and the strategy-versus-position gap.}
A representation that organizes by \emph{where} an agent goes rather than by its
\emph{strategy} is exactly what equivariant networks address: an MDP homomorphic
network is equivariant to symmetries of the state-action
space~\cite{vanderpol2020}, and its multi-agent form notes that ``rotating the
state globally results in a permutation of the optimal joint
policy''~\cite{vanderpol2021}. Euclidean-equivariant actor-critics~\cite{chen2023}
and equivariant learned models~\cite{equivmuzero2023} extend this to cooperative
control and planning, and equivariant graph networks bring 2--5$\times$
generalization gains to MARL on this very benchmark family---with the documented
caveat that naive equivariance can hurt early exploration~\cite{mcclellan2024e2gn2}.
Our arena's four corners carry a dihedral ($D_4$) symmetry,
so a $D_4$-equivariant JEPA encoder---whose strategy latent is invariant to which
corner is targeted---is the principled route to a representation that captures
strategy independently of position; we flag this as the highest-leverage open
direction. A complementary, \emph{learned} route is to pick the intent
representation by its value to the ego agent: mixing multiple intent
representations and maximizing mutual information with the ego agent's future
returns~\cite{odrowaz2026gim}---a natural baseline for the equivariant encoder. When latent abstraction discards control-relevant
detail~\cite{terver2025}, planning through it can fail, which accounts for our
negative result with a JEPA world model.

\paragraph{Belief-space planning.}
Planning over a belief about partially observed agents is classical in interactive
POMDPs, including differentiable instantiations~\cite{han2019} and scalable
type-based reasoning that maintains a belief over discrete opponent types and
plans with Monte-Carlo tree search~\cite{schwartz2023potmmcp}; modern systems
online-update a latent intent belief to feed a tree-search
planner~\cite{huang2024}. Our pipeline has the same shape---infer a belief, then
plan---at small scale, in the JaxMARL~\cite{jaxmarl} reimplementation of the MPE
predator--prey task~\cite{lowe2017}, with the distinguishing features of a
predictive, label-free opponent encoder.

\section{Relation to the proposal and next steps}

This study validates the proposal's loop end to end on its first domain. Part~1:
the opponent's latent strategy is recoverable from its trajectory into a
calibrated belief, and---crucially for co-training---a model that ignores that
uncertainty is brittle to a strategy it did not expect ($-47\%$), the overfitting
failure adaptive opponent modeling targets. Part~2: sampling the
uncertainty-aware opponent model inside a planner produces the most robust best
response against an opponent that varies its strategy every episode, beating even
an oracle that is handed the answer. The natural next steps are exactly the full
proposal: replace the discrete classifier with the variational encoder
$q_\phi(\zlat\mid\tred)$ and a latent-conditioned $\pi^{\mathrm{red}}_\psi$
(behavior cloning, then contrastive preference learning with planner-generated
counterfactuals), and the simulator rollout with a MuZero-style learned model and
a strategy-conditioned value $v^{\mathrm{blue}}(s,\zlat)$, then close the
co-training loop so \red{red} and \blue{blue} adapt to each other.
