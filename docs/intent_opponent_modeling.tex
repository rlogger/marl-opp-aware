\begin{abstract}
Opponent modeling is only worth its cost when (i) the opponent has a genuinely
\emph{hidden} strategy, (ii) that strategy is \emph{inferable} from observed
behavior, (iii) knowing it is \emph{exploitable}, and (iv) the model is
\emph{uncertainty-aware}, so a wrong early guess does not hurt. We construct a
predator--prey task that isolates all four. In a single fixed arena the prey is
assigned, each episode, a hidden intent $\intent$---one of four corners it is
rewarded for haunting---that the predators cannot observe. We find: the intent
strongly shapes the prey's motion (it occupies its corner $\sim$50\% of steps);
a behavioral encoder recovers $\intent$ from the prey's first $k$ steps, with
accuracy rising from $0.37$ ($k{=}3$) to $0.97$ ($k{=}25$) as its posterior
entropy collapses from $1.35$ to $0.03$ nats; and knowing the intent is worth
\textbf{+51\%} captures (an oracle predator that observes $\intent$ catches the
prey $4.05$ times per episode vs.\ $2.68$ for a strong intent-blind hedger). A
predator trained on \emph{perfect} intent is brittle---fed a random intent it
collapses to $1.42$---so we train an \emph{uncertainty-aware} predator on soft
intent beliefs of random sharpness and feed it the encoder's online-sharpening
posterior at test. It \textbf{beats the intent-blind baseline (2.82 vs.\ 2.68)}
and recovers \textbf{53\%} of the oracle's advantage, by hedging while uncertain
and committing once confident. Finally, putting the same belief inside a
one-step-ahead \emph{planner}---scoring joint predator actions by Monte-Carlo
lookahead with the prey's moves sampled from its policy under an intent drawn
from the belief---reaches \textbf{4.31} captures, \textbf{+53\%} over the
reactive belief policy and above the oracle reactive predator; ablating the
belief to flat drops this to $3.07$, so the inferred opponent model supplies
$+40\%$. Inferring a hidden opponent strategy, representing the inference's
uncertainty, and planning against it each measurably improve the best response.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation: what makes opponent modeling worth it}

A planning agent that models its opponent pays a cost: it must infer something
about the opponent and condition on it. That cost is justified only under four
conditions, which prior platforms (including our own earlier experiments) tend to
assume rather than establish:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
\item \textbf{Hidden strategy.} The opponent must vary its behavior along an axis
that is \emph{not} directly readable from a single observation. If the
``strategy'' is induced by a visible change in the environment, an agent can
react to the environment and skip the opponent model entirely.
\item \textbf{Inferable.} The hidden strategy must leave a signature in observed
behavior, recoverable within a useful horizon.
\item \textbf{Exploitable.} Knowing the strategy must materially improve the best
response; otherwise inference is academic.
\item \textbf{Uncertainty-aware.} Inference is uncertain early and sharpens with
observation. A model that commits to a hard early guess can be \emph{worse} than
no model. The agent must represent and act on the belief, not a point estimate.
\end{enumerate}

We design a task in which a single hidden variable---the prey's intent---supplies
(1)--(3) by construction, and we build the (4) uncertainty-aware predator
explicitly. Crucially the environment is \emph{identical} across strategies: the
strategy lives in the opponent, not the map.

\section{Task: a hidden-intent predator--prey game}

\paragraph{Markov game.}
Three predators $P=\{1,2,3\}$ pursue one prey $q$ in a fixed $2$D arena with two
fixed obstacles (JaxMARL MPE dynamics, $5$ discrete actions, $H=25$ step
episodes). At each reset the prey is assigned an intent
$\intent \sim \mathrm{Uniform}\{0,1,2,3\}$ indexing one of four corners
$c_\intent \in \{\pm 0.8\}^2$. Writing $c^t_i = \mathbf{1}[\lVert x^t_i - x^t_q\rVert
< \rho_i+\rho_q]$ for predator $i$ contacting the prey, the team rewards are
\begin{equation}
  r^{\mathrm{pred},t} = \lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}\!\sum_{i\in P} c^t_i,
  \qquad
  r^{\mathrm{prey},t} = -\lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}\!\sum_{i\in P} c^t_i
     \;+\; \lambda_{\mathrm{int}}\,\mathbf{1}\!\big[\lVert x^t_q - c_\intent\rVert < r_{\mathrm{int}}\big],
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}=10$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{int}}=4$,
$r_{\mathrm{int}}=0.35$. So the prey earns a per-step bonus for loitering at its
assigned corner, in addition to evading. Predators are slowed
($v_{\max}=0.75$, accel $2.0$ vs.\ the prey's $1.3/4.0$) so the prey can express
its intent rather than being forced into pure flight. Captures per episode are
$\cps = \lambda_{\mathrm{tag}}^{-1}\sum_t r^{\mathrm{pred},t}$.

\paragraph{Who observes the intent.}
The \textbf{prey always} observes its own intent (one-hot appended to its
observation). The predators observe it according to the condition:
\begin{description}[style=nextline,font=\normalfont\bfseries,leftmargin=1.2em]
\item[unaware] no intent input: the predator must hedge over the four corners.
\item[oracle] the true one-hot $e_\intent$: an upper bound on what intent buys.
\item[belief] a soft belief $\bel = \alpha\, e_\intent + (1-\alpha)\,\tfrac{1}{4}\mathbf{1}$
with sharpness $\alpha \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)$ drawn per episode during
training, so the predator learns to use the intent slot as a \emph{belief}---to
hedge when it is flat and exploit when it is peaked.
\end{description}
Each condition is a separate MAPPO co-training run (centralized critic,
3 seeds, $2\times10^6$ steps); the prey and predators co-adapt.

\section{Method: inference and an uncertainty-aware best response}

\paragraph{Behavioral intent encoder.}
Let $\tau_{0:k} = (x^0_q,\dots,x^{k-1}_q)$ be the prey's first $k$ positions. We
fit a classifier $q_\phi(\intent \mid \tau_{0:k})$ (a one-hidden-layer MLP on the
flattened positions) and read both a hard estimate $\hat\intent = \arg\max
q_\phi$ and the full posterior $\bel_k = q_\phi(\cdot \mid \tau_{0:k})$. We report
$k$-resolved accuracy $a(k)$ and posterior entropy $H(k) = \expect[-\sum_\intent
\bel_k(\intent)\log \bel_k(\intent)]$, the latter being the model's own
uncertainty.

\paragraph{Online belief at test.}
Against a fixed prey, the predator's belief sharpens as the episode unfolds: at
step $t$ we form $\bel_t = q_\phi(\cdot \mid \tau_{0:k(t)})$ using the largest
trained window $k(t) \le t{+}1$ (flat $\tfrac14\mathbf{1}$ before the first
window). The \textbf{belief} predator---trained on soft beliefs---consumes
$\bel_t$ directly. We compare five intent signals fed to the predator:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
signal & predator / meaning \\
\midrule
guess     & oracle predator, a \emph{random} fixed intent (model with no inference) \\
inferred  & oracle predator, hard $\hat\intent$ from $k{=}8$ steps (commits to a guess) \\
unaware   & intent-blind predator (no model; strong hedger baseline) \\
belief    & uncertainty-aware predator fed the online posterior $\bel_t$ (\textbf{ours}) \\
oracle    & oracle predator, true $\intent$ (ceiling) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\section{Results}

\paragraph{The intent is hidden, expressed, and inferable.}
Figure~\ref{fig:main}(left) shows the prey's occupancy coloured by intent: four
clusters at the four corners, in one fixed arena---structure a predator cannot
read off a single frame but can infer over time. The encoder
(Figure~\ref{fig:main}, middle; Table~\ref{tab:enc}) climbs from near chance at
$k{=}3$ to $0.97$ by the episode's end, its posterior entropy collapsing from
$1.35$ to $0.03$ nats.

\paragraph{Knowing the intent is highly exploitable; a naive model is brittle.}
Table~\ref{tab:ladder} is the capture ladder. Observing the true intent lifts the
predator from the intent-blind $2.68$ to $4.05$ captures/episode---a
\textbf{+51\%} gain, so intent is very much worth knowing. But that oracle
predator is brittle: fed a \emph{random} intent it collapses to $1.42$, well
below the intent-blind hedger, because it learned to rely on a signal it now
cannot trust. Feeding it a hard inferred intent at $k{=}8$ recovers most of the
damage ($2.56$) but still does not beat the hedger---committing to a single guess
inherits the encoder's $26\%$ error.

\paragraph{Representing uncertainty is what makes inference pay.}
The uncertainty-aware \textbf{belief} predator---trained on soft beliefs and fed
the online posterior---\emph{beats} the intent-blind baseline
($2.82$ vs.\ $2.68$) and recovers $53\%$ of the oracle-over-guess gap. It wins
where the hard-inferred predator does not because it hedges while $\bel_t$ is
flat (behaving like the robust hedger early) and commits only as $\bel_t$
sharpens (approaching the oracle late). The ordering
$\text{guess} < \text{inferred} \approx \text{unaware} < \text{belief} <
\text{oracle}$ is the entire thesis of uncertainty-aware opponent modeling on one
axis: inference helps, but only if the agent represents how much it knows.

\section{Planning with the belief}

The belief predator of the previous section is \emph{reactive}: it maps the
current belief to an action. We now make the belief drive a one-step-ahead
\emph{plan}. At each decision the predators hold the online posterior $\bel_t$
and score every candidate joint action $u \in \{0,\dots,4\}^3$ by Monte-Carlo
lookahead through the true simulator, with the prey's moves drawn from its own
policy under an intent sampled from the belief:
\begin{equation}
  Q(s_t, u) = \expect_{\substack{\intent \sim \bel_t}}
  \!\left[\;\sum_{h=0}^{H-1} \gamma^h\, r^{\mathrm{pred}}_{t+h}
  \;-\; w\,\min_{i\in P}\lVert x^{t+H}_i - x^{t+H}_q \rVert
  \;\middle|\; u_t{=}u,\;
  a^q_{t+h} \sim \pi^q(\cdot\mid s_{t+h}, \intent),\;
  a^P_{t+h>0} \sim \pi^{\mathrm{bel}}(\cdot\mid s_{t+h}, \bel_t)\right],
  \label{eq:plan}
\end{equation}
and act greedily, $u_t = \arg\max_u Q(s_t, u)$. The first term is the in-horizon
discounted captures; the second is a leaf term ($w{=}1$, $H{=}5$, $\gamma{=}0.95$)
that rewards ending close to the \emph{imagined future} prey---which, under a
sampled intent, sits at the believed corner---so the predators plan an
interception rather than waiting for the rare in-horizon capture. The opponent
model is the prey's own trained policy $\pi^q$ conditioned on the sampled intent;
the dynamics model is the real simulator. Sampled intents are shared across
candidates per rollout (a paired comparison), so the arg-max is low-variance with
$K{=}8$ rollouts.

Table~\ref{tab:plan} and Figure~\ref{fig:plan} give the result. The
belief-conditioned planner reaches \textbf{4.31} captures/episode---\textbf{+53\%}
over the reactive belief policy, and it \emph{exceeds} the oracle \emph{reactive}
predator ($4.05$): planning interceptions beats reacting, even against an opponent
whose intent is known. The control is decisive: a planner fed a \emph{flat} belief
(lookahead with no opponent inference) reaches only $3.07$, so the inferred belief
contributes \textbf{+40\%}---the lookahead alone is not what wins, the
belief-conditioned opponent model is.

\section{Toward a learned dynamics model}

The Part 2 planner uses the true simulator as its dynamics model. A deployed
agent may not have one, so we ask whether the same plan works through a
\emph{learned} model. We learn the agents' physics transition
$f_\theta:(x^{1:4}, v^{1:4}, a^{1:4}) \mapsto (x'^{1:4}, v'^{1:4})$ from
$10^5$ transitions by \emph{rollout training} (unrolling $f_\theta$ four steps
and matching the true four-step trajectory, which keeps compounding error small),
and run the planner of Eq.~\eqref{eq:plan} through $f_\theta$ in place of the
simulator; observations and the capture/leaf terms remain geometric readouts of
the predicted state. The model is accurate---position RMSE $0.004$ at one step
and $0.072$ at five steps, on an arena $3.2$ wide.

Yet the learned-model planner reaches only $2.53 \pm 0.34$ captures/episode---it
recovers $59\%$ of the true-simulator planner ($4.31$) and roughly matches the
reactive belief policy. The dynamics are accurate \emph{on-policy}, but the
planner scores all $125$ joint actions, many off the model's training
distribution, and the arg-max \emph{exploits} the model's optimistic errors over
the five-step closed loop---the canonical model-exploitation failure of
model-based RL, sharpened here because discrete policies amplify small state
errors into different actions. We also implemented the standard remedy---a short
two-step lookahead with a learned belief-conditioned value
$V_\psi(s, \bel)$ at the leaf---but the value was too coarse
($\mathrm{MSE}\approx 0.6$ on returns spanning $0$--$4$) and made matters worse
($0.76$). Closing the gap is a model-fidelity and model-exploitation problem:
ensembles with disagreement penalties, on-policy/DAgger model refinement against
the planner's own action queries, and a sharper value are the levers. We report
this as an honest intermediate result: planning through a learned model is
\emph{feasible} here---the planner runs with no simulator at test time and stays
on par with the reactive policy---but matching the true-simulator planner needs a
model the planner cannot exploit.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why this design, and not the obvious one.}
A tempting alternative induces ``strategies'' by changing the environment (e.g.\
moving where resources sit) and training a separate policy per layout. But then
the strategy is visible in the observation and the opponent model is partly
circular---an agent can condition on the environment and skip it. The hidden
intent here is invisible in any single frame; it can \emph{only} be had by
modeling the opponent's behavior over time. That is the regime the method is for.

\paragraph{The magnitude, honestly.}
The uncertainty-aware gain over the intent-blind hedger is real but modest
($+5\%$) at $H=25$ steps, because much of an episode is spent before the belief
sharpens; the gain concentrates in the later, confident steps and would grow with
horizon. The large, unambiguous numbers are the \emph{value} of intent
($+51\%$ oracle vs.\ unaware) and the \emph{brittleness} of a naive model
($-47\%$ when its intent input is wrong). Together they motivate exactly the
uncertainty-aware treatment: the prize is large, and the failure mode of ignoring
uncertainty is severe.

\paragraph{From reaction to planning.}
The reactive belief predator gains $+5\%$ over the intent-blind hedger; putting
the same belief inside a one-step-ahead plan (Eq.~\ref{eq:plan}) gains $+53\%$ and
overtakes even the oracle reactive policy. The belief is the same; what changes is
that the planner uses it to \emph{simulate the opponent forward} and intercept,
rather than to colour a reaction. Swapping the real simulator for a learned
dynamics model keeps the same logic and runs without a simulator at test time,
but only partly recovers the planner's gain because the planner exploits model
error on out-of-distribution actions---a model-fidelity problem we report
honestly rather than tune away.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The leaf term (Eq.~\ref{eq:plan}) is a hand-specified shaping toward the imagined
prey; a learned belief-conditioned value would replace it. The planner uses the
real simulator, which the deployed setting may not have. And the whole study lives
in a balanced regime tuned so the prey can express its intent---the operating
point at which opponent modeling is meaningful---rather than a regime where the
prey is dominated and has no strategy to infer.
